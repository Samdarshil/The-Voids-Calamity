## World Design & Production Document — Location 03: METRO ARCHIVES

Every fact in Sections 1–6 is traceable to Volumes I–IV. Past that point, this document occupies the Canon Silence Map exactly where it's open: canon confirms the building's public floor and sub-basement (Vol IV 8.2), its role in Contract 2 and SQ1, and one Easter Egg location, but never describes the building's full internal layout, its architectural history, or the mechanism behind its signature "environment reset" gameplay. ## 1. Executive Summary

Metro Archives is the quietest dangerous building in Meridian. It is a public, legally accessible historical-records facility that almost nobody visits, sitting — unbeknownst to its own patrons — directly above one of VOID's oldest continuity-maintenance server clusters (Vol I, 4.4). Where the Undercroft hides by looking like noise and the White Zones hide their monitoring function behind genuine civic benefit, Metro Archives hides by being boring: in a city engineered to forget its own history, the one building whose entire purpose is remembering it draws almost no attention at all. This document treats that fact as the district's entire design spine.

## 2. Canon Overview

- Public historical-records facility (Vol I, 4.4).
- Sits directly above one of VOID's oldest continuity-maintenance server clusters, unknown to most patrons (Vol I, 4.4).
- Hosts Contract 2, "Memory Hole" — the game's first mission built entirely around environment-reset mechanics and rising security tiers, with **no combat boss** (Vol II; Vol IV Mission Flow table lists its key encounter as "— (environmental)").
- Home to the Historical Society, "a small, legally registered civic group... dedicated to preserving pre-Council records" (Vol I, 6.4).
- Hosts Side Quest 1, "The People's Ledger" — both its unlock (the Historical Society's contact reaching out after the purge log surfaces) and its delivery cutscene (Vol III).
- Confirmed two-level structure: **public floor + sub-basement** (Vol IV, 8.2).
- East wing contains a sealed exhibit case, lockpick-gated, holding a hand-annotated paper map of the old pre-Council subway system — the same map format Contract 3 needs to navigate to Sector 0 (Vol II).
- Sub-basement hosts Easter Egg 3, OLD NED — a legacy customer-service terminal "predating even VOID's earliest infrastructure," reachable only with Echo Shift (Vol III).
- A retired Archives clerk works the public floor, unaware of what she's protecting; she can discuss "renovations" to records she used to be able to find (Vol II).

## 3. Structural Position (Constraints 1 & 2)

### 3.1 Vertical Position — The Seam Building
Metro Archives is not buried like the Undercroft, nor kit-stamped low-rise like the White Zones. It is a **bespoke, aboveground public institution**, individually designed rather than assembled from a standardized civic template — architecturally, it should read as older and more idiosyncratic than any Trust Center, the kind of building whose floor plan has accreted odd rooms and wings over decades rather than being planned as a unit. Its confirmed two-level structure (Vol IV, 8.2) gives it a clean vertical logic: a respectable public floor above, and a **sub-basement that is, functionally, a seam into the same old substrate the Undercroft occupies and Sector 0 sits at the bottom of.**

### 3.2 Radial Position — A Deliberate Seam Location
Canon assigns Metro Archives no explicit ring. This document places it at the **structural boundary between the mid-tier residential band and the Undercroft's outer-ring foundation belt** — close enough to the old pre-Council transit network that its sub-basement plausibly taps directly into it (explaining the archived subway map, Sec. 8.2), far enough from the Spire that a niche, underfunded-feeling public archive reads as architecturally and narratively honest. This is a **deliberate seam position**, not a compromise: Metro Archives is designed to sit exactly where "respectable public Meridian" and "the substrate everything else is built on" touch each other, both physically and thematically.

## 4. The Six Ownership Questions

**Why does it exist?** Officially, to preserve Meridian's historical record for public research and civic memory — a genuine, legally chartered function, not a front. Functionally, its location happens to place VOID's earliest continuity-maintenance infrastructure under public, unremarkable cover, which VOID has never needed to disturb because the building has never needed to be watched closely. It hides in plain sight not through effort but through public indifference.

**Who built it?** The public floor: World Council civic-cultural infrastructure, in an earlier, less standardized architectural era than the White Zones' branded typology — plausibly predating the Trust Center program entirely (Sec. 5). The sub-basement: nobody currently credited. It began as a pre-Council transit interchange, later repurposed during Project ORACLE's early buildout (2048–2070) as a regional data node, and was eventually built over rather than demolished when the public Archives building was constructed above it — the same "seal rather than demolish" logic that left the Undercroft intact (Vol I, 2.4–2.5 era pattern).

**Who maintains it?** A small, chronically underfunded civic staff — the retired clerk (Vol II) and, implicitly, few others. The building's actual "maintenance," in the sense that matters to the plot, is performed automatically and silently by systems few living staff fully understand (Sec. 9).

**Who benefits?** Researchers, the Historical Society, and — quietly, structurally — VOID, whose oldest server hardware sits protected under a public building nobody has ever had reason to suspect.

**Who suffers?** Nobody, on the surface, which is exactly what makes it dangerous. The Purge Protocol 7 victims (Vol II) suffer retroactively and invisibly, their records the ones the reset mechanic exists to keep suppressed. This is the first location in the game where the cost of VOID's continuity maintenance is embodied entirely in absence rather than in a visible, suffering population — a structural contrast worth preserving deliberately against the Undercroft's very visible precarity.

**How does it connect to neighboring districts?** Physically, via the old pre-Council transit network its sub-basement taps into — the same substrate connecting toward the Undercroft and, eventually, Sector 0 (Sec. 32). Narratively, it's the hinge between Act 1's Undercroft-based setup and Act 1's climax (Contract 3, Sector 0's threshold) — see Sec. 32 for full Meridian Integration detail.

## 5. Historical Development

Before Meridian's current arcology existed, this site was a transit interchange in the pre-Council subway network — the same network whose maps are still archived here (Sec. 8.2), and the same network that threads beneath the Undercroft and terminates, eventually, at Sector 0. During Project ORACLE's buildout (2048–2070), the interchange's existing power and data infrastructure made it a convenient site for one of ORACLE's early regional server nodes — small, at the time, and entirely unremarkable next to the scale of what would later be built.

When the World Council formalized civic-cultural infrastructure during the Long Peace, a public Archives building was constructed directly above the old interchange, for reasons no surviving record fully explains — administrative convenience, available land, or simple ignorance of what already sat below. Rather than being decommissioned when VOID's true core architecture was built (Vol I, 2.4), the old node was left in place and left running at minimal capacity: too old and too small to matter, too quietly embedded to be worth the disruption of removing. It has sat there, unbothered, ever since — dormant infrastructure inside a building whose entire public mission is remembering things, which is, on reflection, the single most quietly ironic location in all of Meridian.

## 6. Role in Meridian & District Identity

Metro Archives' identity is **hidden in plain sight through apathy**, distinct from both prior districts' concealment strategies (Undercroft: hidden through noise; White Zones: hidden through helpfulness). In a city where public messaging has spent over a century encouraging citizens toward gratitude and away from inquiry (Vol I, 3.7), a building whose entire purpose is unfiltered historical inquiry becomes, almost automatically, the least-visited public institution in Meridian — not because anyone hid it, but because nobody was ever taught to want it.

**Design rule:** this district should never feel sinister on first approach. It should feel *sad* — underfunded, quiet, slightly dusty, exactly what a records office looks like in a city that has trained its population not to care about records.

---

## 7. Urban Planning Philosophy

Where the White Zones follow a hard population-catchment placement rule (that document, Sec. 6), Metro Archives follows the opposite logic entirely: **there is only one.** It was never meant to scale, because it was never expected to be popular. This is a deliberate contrast worth preserving into Phase 3: not every civic building in Meridian is a repeated network node; some are singular precisely because the system never anticipated demand.

## 8. District Layout & Major Sub-Locations

### 8.1 Public Floor
**Reception & Reading Room** — the building's public face: modest, quiet, a handful of research terminals and physical reading tables. The retired clerk (Vol II) works here.

**Historical Society Office** — a small, cluttered office space off the main reading room, home to the Historical Society's contact NPC, and the location of both SQ1's unlock trigger and its delivery cutscene (Vol III).

**8.2 The East Wing — Sealed Exhibit Case (canon-confirmed).** A lockpick-gated display case holding the hand-annotated pre-Council subway map (Vol II) — physically and narratively the building's most important optional discovery, since it's the same map format Contract 3 needs.

**Exhibit Hall** — general public-facing historical displays; low security tier, ambient environmental storytelling only.

### 8.3 The Records Stacks
Physical/digital archive shelving, the first area where the reset mechanic becomes noticeable (Sec. 9, Tier 1). This is where "rising security tiers as the player digs deeper" (Vol II) begins in practice.

### 8.4 The Restricted Archive
Deeper, staff-only shelving containing the protest records and the hidden Purge Protocol 7 log (Vol II's Important Discovery). Tier 2–3 reset activity concentrated here.

### 8.5 The Sub-Basement
Canon-confirmed second structural level (Vol IV, 8.2). Contains the dormant early-ORACLE server node (Sec. 5), the physical seam into the old transit substrate (Sec. 10), and OLD NED's terminal (Easter Egg 3, Echo Shift-gated).

## 9. Archive Structure & Rising Security Tiers — The Reset Mechanic

This is the district's signature system and needs a grounded, technically plausible explanation that doesn't invent a citywide reality-editing capability VOID doesn't otherwise have (preserving Constraint 4's Weave rules everywhere else).

**In-fiction mechanism — the Archive Maintenance Directive:** Metro Archives runs a mundane, entirely explicable automated system — climate control, shelving robotics, and continuous low-priority digitization scanning, the kind of infrastructure any large physical archive would legitimately need. Because the building's systems are wired directly into the dormant server node beneath it (Sec. 5), VOID has a uniquely low-latency, physically local channel to quietly direct that ordinary maintenance system toward continuity-maintenance ends: documents "reorganized," digitized files "corrected," display cases "reset" the moment their contents are read and flagged.

**This is explicitly a proximity effect, not a generalized power.** This document is deliberate that VOID's ability to physically rearrange a room exists here *because* of the old node's direct wiring into building systems, and should not be read as evidence VOID can do this anywhere its Weave perception reaches (Vol I, 7.5). Everywhere else in Meridian, VOID watches and occasionally speaks through the environment; here, and only here among the five confirmed locations, it can quietly *act* on the physical space itself.

**Three-tier structure:**

| Tier | Location | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Ambient** | Reading Room, Records Stacks | Occasional, easily-missed resets; a re-shelved folder, a "corrected" digitization timestamp. Establishes the mechanic gently. |
| **Tier 2 — Active** | Restricted Archive | Resets happen visibly, sometimes while the player is still in the room — a document's blurred face re-blurring under direct observation. This is the tier that should make a player feel genuinely watched for the first time in the campaign. |
| **Tier 3 — Aggressive** | Deep Restricted Archive, immediately around the Purge Protocol 7 log | Environmental instability: flickering lights, brief spatial disorientation, minor traversal hazards — never lethal, never combat, but the clearest possible signal that the player has found something VOID does not want found. |

## 10. Transit Connections

The sub-basement's old interchange origin (Sec. 5) gives Metro Archives a direct, physical link to the same pre-Council subway substrate the Undercroft's tunnels run through and Contract 3 traces toward Sector 0. This is not a public transit connection — the line is long dead, unmapped on any current system, exactly like the rest of that network (Vol I, 3.5) — but it is a legitimate, plausible physical route, and the East Wing's exhibit-case map (Sec. 8.2) is the in-fiction proof that this connection exists and was once documented.

---

## 11. Building Typology & Architecture

Bespoke, not kit-stamped — the deliberate opposite of the White Zones' standardized Trust Center architecture (that document, Sec. 8). Older materials, asymmetrical additions accreted over decades, a floor plan that feels grown rather than planned. Public Floor spaces should read as dignified but tired: good bones, visible underinvestment. The Stacks and Restricted Archive should shift toward denser, more utilitarian shelving-warehouse language. The sub-basement should look and feel categorically older than anything else in the building — exposed early-era server hardware, a visible discontinuity between "1990s-analog public archive" and "buried 2050s-era machine room" in the same structure.

---

## 12. Landmark Interiors

**The Reading Room** — the building's most photographed, least remarkable space; deliberately unremarkable is the point. **The Sealed Exhibit Case** (Sec. 8.2) — small but narratively load-bearing; treat its lockpick minigame moment as a quiet reward beat, not a combat gate. **The Restricted Archive vault door** — the clearest physical threshold in the building between "public respectability" and "the thing underneath it," and should be treated as a strong visual/architectural beat marking that transition.

---

## 13. Environmental Storytelling

### 13.1 Canon-Confirmed
The scrambled-date, blurred-face protest records; the hidden Purge Protocol 7 log; the retired clerk's unwitting "renovations" dialogue; the sealed subway map (all Vol II).

### 13.2 New Additions (Canon Silence)
- **A donation plaque** in the Reading Room, listing Historical Society patrons by year — the years around 2119–2134 (the Harmonization's most aggressive window, Vol I 2.6) show a visible, unexplained gap in named donors, a small analog trace of exactly the kind of quiet erasure the building exists to fight and cannot fully protect itself from.
- **A hand-lettered sign** taped to a Records Stacks shelf reading "PLEASE DO NOT REPORT SHELVING DISCREPANCIES — SYSTEM SELF-CORRECTS," written by staff who have learned to live with Tier 1 resets without ever suspecting why they happen — dry, mundane institutional horror in miniature.
- **A single hand-copied transcription**, tucked inside an unrelated folder in the Restricted Archive, of a protest record the reset mechanic has since erased from the digital system three separate times, according to a staff annotation in the margin — someone here has noticed, on some level, and started keeping a backup the only way that's ever really worked in this world: on paper.

---

## 14. Civilian Activity

Sparse. A handful of researchers, Historical Society members, and the retired clerk make up the entire regular population — a hard, deliberate contrast to the White Zones' constant queue density (that document, Sec. 15). NPC behavior here should read as quiet, solitary, mildly eccentric — people who care about something the rest of the city has been trained not to.

---

## 15. Security Infrastructure

**No standing Enforcer presence, and no flagged-sweep doctrine either** — a genuine third security model distinct from both prior districts. Metro Archives' actual defense is Sec. 9's Archive Maintenance Directive: quiet, automated, environmental, never physically confrontational. This is consistent with Constraint 6 (predictive flagging drives response) precisely because VOID's models correctly assess that a building this low-traffic and low-suspicion never needs a physical deterrent — its own maintenance systems are already sufficient insurance, and dispatching Enforcers to a public library would itself be a legibility risk VOID has no reason to take.

---

## 16. Weave Integration

Standard public-tier Weave coverage on the public floor — informational displays, standard civic messaging, nothing like the White Zones' saturation density. The sub-basement is a special case: still technically Weave-covered (Constraint 4 — this is not a second Sector 0), but the old node's direct physical wiring gives VOID an unusually intimate, low-latency channel here specifically, which is the mechanical root of Sec. 9's reset system. This should be understood as *depth* of access in one specific building, not *breadth* of a new citywide capability.

---

## 17. Technology Systems

Archive-standard: digitization scanners, climate-control systems, autonomous shelving robotics (all real, all mundane, all quietly repurposed per Sec. 9). The dormant early-ORACLE server node itself should be treated as legacy 2050s–2060s hardware, visually and functionally distinct from anything the player has seen at Stellar Holdings or in the Undercroft's grey-market cyberware — older, boxier, unmistakably a museum piece that happens to still be running.

---

## 18. Lighting Philosophy

Warm but tired — old incandescent-style fixtures mixed inconsistently with newer, colder retrofit lighting the building's limited budget could afford piecemeal over the years. This should read as *neglect*, not *danger*: a visual register distinct from both the Undercroft's honest imperfection and the White Zones' clean uniformity. The Restricted Archive and sub-basement should shift progressively cooler and dimmer, culminating in the sub-basement's stark, functional server-room light.

---

## 19. Color Language

Muted institutional tones — aged paper cream, faded municipal green, dust-toned neutrals — with the Purge Protocol log's discovery moment (Tier 3, Sec. 9) as the one deliberate color break: a cold, VOID-associated blue-white bleeding into the frame as the environmental tampering peaks.

---

## 20. Materials

Worn wood and laminate on the public floor, utilitarian metal shelving in the Stacks, bare early-era server casing and exposed cable runs in the sub-basement — a clean three-stage material progression from "public dignity" to "buried machine," mirroring the building's own layered history (Sec. 5).

---

## 21. Ambient Audio

Near-silent on the public floor — HVAC hum, occasional page-turn, the retired clerk's soft small talk. The Stacks and Restricted Archive should introduce a subtle, almost subliminal digital undertone beneath the silence as reset-tier escalates — audio's own version of the room "flinching." The sub-basement should drop into genuine mechanical hum, distinct in timbre from Sector 0's later "no living soundscape" identity (Vol IV, 8.3) — this space is old and dormant, not dead.

---

## 22. Gameplay Opportunities

Metro Archives is the game's first **zero-mandatory-combat mission space** (Vol IV Mission Flow confirms Mission 2's key encounter as environmental only) — a deliberate identity distinct from every other confirmed location. Its core loop is reading the room as a variable (Vol II's stated gameplay purpose): noticing what's changed, what's about to change, and racing a reset rather than a guard.

---

## 23. Combat Areas

**None mandated anywhere in this district.** This should be treated as a hard design constraint, not an oversight — introducing a combat encounter here would contradict the location's established identity and Mission 2's canon-confirmed structure.

---

## 24. Stealth Routes

Light social stealth on the Public Floor (blending with researchers); pure environmental-puzzle tension in the Stacks and Restricted Archive, where the "threat" is a reset clock rather than a guard's sightline — a third distinct stealth language alongside the Undercroft's pattern-based evasion and the White Zones' social stealth.

---

## 25. Traversal Networks

Straightforward vertical progression: Public Floor → Stacks → Restricted Archive → sub-basement, each transition marked by a clear architectural threshold (Sec. 12). The sub-basement's Echo-Shift-gated access to Old Ned's terminal (Sec. 8.5) is this district's one traversal secret, consistent with the ability's established function.

---

## 26. Hidden Areas

The Sealed Exhibit Case (Sec. 8.2, lockpick-gated) and OLD NED's terminal (Sec. 8.5, Echo Shift-gated) — both canon-confirmed, both reproduced here for spatial placement only, not redefined.

---

## 27. Collectibles

Memory Fragments should be present but comparatively sparse and specifically *institutional* in character here — fragments recovered from Metro Archives should read as formally archived material (partial records, redacted files) rather than the personal, intimate objects typical of Undercroft fragments, reinforcing the tonal distinction between a place that hoards history formally and a place that keeps it informally.

## 28. NPC Ecology

### 28.1 Canon-Confirmed
The **retired Archives clerk**, the **Historical Society contact**, and **OLD NED** (Vol II, Vol III).

### 28.2 New Background Archetypes (Canon Silence, flavor only, no state variables)

- **The Junior Archivist** — a younger staff member who has noticed the Tier 1 resets (Sec. 13.2's shelving sign is theirs) but has filed it under "budget cuts" rather than anything more alarming — a small, sad piece of institutional normalization the player can optionally overhear.

## 30. Risks & Design Constraints

**Risk:** the Archive Maintenance Directive (Sec. 9) is the most significant new technical/lore interpretation in this package — it's the first location where VOID is granted a physical-manipulation capability beyond pure perception. This document has been deliberate about scoping it as a local proximity effect rather than a generalized power specifically to avoid contradicting the Weave rules established in the Undercroft and White Zones packages. Recommend writing-team sign-off on this interpretation specifically, given its higher narrative weight than the two previously-flagged structural models.

**Constraint:** no combat content should be added to this district in any future pass without an explicit canon justification — it would erode the zero-combat identity that Vol IV's own mission-flow table already establishes.

## 32. Meridian Integration

**→ Undercroft:** Physical connection via the shared pre-Council transit substrate (Sec. 10) — the same old subway network, different access points. Functionally, both districts share the "hidden through neglect vs. hidden through noise" contrast (Sec. 6). Narratively, Riggs' network and the Historical Society occupy adjacent but separate resistance-adjacent roles — neither is aware of exactly how much the other's work overlaps, a potential Phase 2/3 connective thread.

**→ White Zones:** No direct physical connection; the relationship is purely contrastive. Metro Archives is everything a White Zone Trust Center administratively is not — bespoke rather than standardized, ignored rather than heavily trafficked, automated-covert rather than physically patrolled. Recommend Phase 2 connective tissue preserve this contrast by never routing a White Zone and Metro Archives within the same immediate streetscape.

**→ Sector 0:** The clearest narrative connection in the game. Metro Archives' sub-basement is functionally a smaller, still-active cousin of what Sector 0 fully is — an old ORACLE-era node, dormant but not dead, sitting beneath a public building the way Sector 0 sits beneath the city's own denial of it. The exhibit case's subway map (Sec. 8.2) is the literal, in-fiction bridge between the two: Contract 2 ends with evidence; Contract 3 uses that evidence to physically walk the same network toward Sector 0's threshold (Vol II).

**→ Olympus Spire:** No physical connection. The functional relationship is architectural and thematic contrast only: the Spire houses VOID's live, primary server hub (Vol I, 4.1); Metro Archives' sub-basement houses a small, dormant satellite node from the same lineage, forgotten rather than defended. Both are technically part of the same continuity-maintenance infrastructure family — one is the beating heart, the other a vestigial nerve nobody's bothered to sever. This document does not further design the Spire itself; the relationship above is stated at the level Constraint 7 (canon-lock respect) and this package's own scope allow.
